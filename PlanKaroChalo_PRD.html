Section 1

Discovery Insights

This product is grounded in primary research across 132+ unique data points: 27 interviews spanning structured deep conversations, consolidated Q&A-format interviews, qualitative retrospective write-ups, and cross-group interviews — plus 105 survey responses across three independent surveys. Respondents covered friend groups, multi-family trips, women's treks, couples groups, motorcycle touring clubs, and three-generation family vacations. Group sizes ranged from 2-3 to 22+. The full interview registry is available in the Research Appendix (linked separately).

Secondary research validated primary findings against published market data: the global group travel market reached ~$168B in 2024 at 7.2% CAGR, India's travel market was valued at $22.5B with 2.5 billion domestic tourist visits in 2023 (up 45% YoY), and Asia Pacific leads global growth at 8.2–8.4% CAGR.

The trip lifecycle has eight stages: spark → dates → destination → budget → accommodation → itinerary → on-trip → post-trip. Across every data source, the critical failure point is stages 2-4. In the 40-person survey, "coordinating dates and availability" was the most time-consuming stage for roughly half the respondents, and "dates couldn't be aligned" was the #1 reason trips didn't happen — cited by 14 of 40 respondents.

But dates are just the entry point to a deeper coordination failure. One interviewee — a frustrated non-lead who travels infrequently but cares deeply about trip quality — described the pattern: excitement in the WhatsApp group, then 7-8 destination suggestions simultaneously, a poll with 3 voters, preferences debated with no structure, people stop responding, the group goes silent, and the trip quietly dies through inertia. The 40-person survey confirmed: 20% of failed trips died from "planning became too overwhelming and momentum died."

The organizer tax is structural. In the 49-person survey, 55% said "1-2 people did most of it," another 12% said one person did everything. One super-organizer plans all 5-6 trips per year for his group — when he became unavailable, the trip was cancelled entirely. A family coordinator described managing seven people across three generations: "None of that is hard, it's just a lot of small things all in my head and nobody else is thinking about them."

A college-age organizer echoed the same for friend groups: "I end up doing it because otherwise it won't happen." He'd made a Google Sheet nobody opened, then answered the same questions in WhatsApp.

Both cross-group interviewees independently rejected the same features: expense tracking ("the real pain is 'why is this taking 2 weeks to decide dates,' not 'how do we split ₹1,200'"), in-app chat ("WhatsApp is where we talk"), and feature-heavy platforms ("If I see itinerary builder, expense tracker, booking integrations — I'll just ignore all of it").

Section 2

Problem Prioritization

Twelve distinct pain points emerged. After validating against secondary data and the competitive landscape, four are selected for V1 — they sit in the kill zone (stages 2-4), are high-frequency and high-severity, and have no adequate solution in market.

The Trip Journey — Where It Breaks

1

Spark

"Let's go somewhere!" in WhatsApp. No structure, 12 destinations across 3 conversations.

2

Date Alignment KILL ZONE

Manual availability checking. 2-4 weeks of back-and-forth. Trip stalls here most often.

3

Destination Decision KILL ZONE

WhatsApp debates, expired polls, decision paralysis. Trips get canceled during discussion.

4

Budget Agreement KILL ZONE

Rarely discussed explicitly. Silent misalignment surfaces later as dropouts.

5

Accommodation

One person researches across 3-5 OTAs. Siloed, competing preferences.

6

Itinerary

3-4 days of calls + blog research. Massive time sink. AI commoditizing this.

7

On-Trip

Organizer becomes GPS + concierge + accountant. Activity splits, food friction.

8

Post-Trip

Expense settlement drags. Small amounts leak. Splitwise friction.

Critical insight: If a group survives past budget agreement (stage 4), the trip almost always happens. The highest-leverage intervention is collapsing stages 2-4 into a single, structured flow.

Problem Statement

The "default planner" in Indian friend groups and families of 4-10 people needs a way to move their group from "let's go somewhere" to "dates locked, destination chosen, everyone confirmed" because the pre-booking coordination phase is entirely unstructured — currently managed through weeks of WhatsApp back-and-forth, manually piecing together availability, chasing non-responders, and absorbing all cognitive load alone — resulting in 35% of planned trips never happening, organizer burnout across 5-15+ hours per trip, late bookings at higher prices, and compounding social friction.
Section 3

Proposed Solution

Plan Karo Chalo is a link-based trip coordination tool that guides Indian groups through the pre-booking decision sequence — dates → destination → commitment — via a shared dashboard, with no app download required.

That single sentence is what a user should be able to repeat back. It's not a travel planner, not an itinerary builder, not a booking platform. It's the coordination layer that gets a group from "let's go somewhere" to "we're going."

How It Solves Each Pain Point

📅 Date Alignment
Organizer creates a trip, sets candidate date ranges, and shares a link. Members tap their availability via the link — no account needed. System auto-calculates the best overlap window for the target trip duration and surfaces it with a "Lock dates" action. Organizer can also enter constraints on behalf of members who won't use the tool (proxy input).
📊 Trip Status & Momentum
Single-screen dashboard shows the current trip stage (dates → destination → committed → ready), what's decided, what's pending, and who hasn't responded. Progress bar visualizes how close the trip is to locked. "Waiting on 3 people" indicator removes the ambiguity that lets trips quietly die.
🎒 Organizer Tax Reduction
Organizer's only job: create trip + share link. System auto-sends reminders to non-responders. Dashboard eliminates repeating information. Proxy input handles tech-averse members. The tool does the chasing, not the human.
📍 Destination Convergence
After dates lock, the dashboard advances to destination. Any member can propose options. Members vote. Results visible in real-time. Organizer locks when consensus is clear. Simple, structured, with finality.

Responsive web app, mobile-first. React + Supabase + Vercel. Open Gaps

Resolved  Link-based anonymous participation
Supabase Anonymous Sign-Ins (launched Apr 2024) handles this natively. signInAnonymously() creates a real user with unique ID and JWT, without requiring any personal information. Anonymous users get full RLS enforcement and Realtime access. Two caveats: session doesn't persist across devices (acceptable for MVP), and Cloudflare Turnstile should be enabled post-launch.
Open  Will users adopt a tool alongside WhatsApp?
Working assumption: yes — if link-based, under 30 seconds, and reduces WhatsApp noise. Fallback: WhatsApp-formatted summaries make the tool invisible to resistant members.
Open  Will the organizer persona pay?
64% said they'd pay ₹49-500+. Splitwise Pro ($3/mo) and Wanderlog Pro ($5/mo) establish willingness. Fallback: freemium — core coordination stays free.
Open  Does the lock mechanism feel too rigid?
Working bet: groups want clarity over flexibility. Fallback: "soft lock" (reversible with group consent) can supplement hard lock.
Section 5

Build Guide — Design Decisions

The choices that define this product aren't the features — they're the tradeoffs. Five decisions shaped the architecture, and each involved rejecting a plausible alternative.

Link-based participation — no account for members
Rejected: Require all members to create accounts
Every interviewee said the same thing: asking people to install an app or create an account is where adoption dies. The family coordinator's father "still calls to ask how to download a PDF." Howbout (closest competitor on dates) requires universal app install — that's their bottleneck and our differentiator.
Stage-gate progression — dates → destination → commitment
Rejected: Freeform dashboard, any decision in any order
Flexibility is what WhatsApp already provides, and it produces chaos. One interviewee described 7-8 destination suggestions flooding in before dates were even discussed. Stage gates enforce the discipline groups need but cannot self-impose.
Organizer-created trips — one person sets it up
Rejected: Equal co-creation by all members
67% of groups have 1-2 planners. The only distributed planning counter-example was a 4-person motorcycle group. Designing for "equal participation" sounds ideal but ignores reality. Others participate through responding, voting, and confirming.
Automated nudging — system sends reminders
Rejected: Manual "remind" button for organizer
The organizer tax isn't just time — it's the social awkwardness of chasing friends and family. If the system sends the nudge, the social cost shifts from the organizer to the tool. "The group is waiting on your input" is neutral accountability, not personal pressure.
Visible individual response status
Rejected: Only aggregate numbers ("4 of 7 responded")
The college-age organizer: "Show me 'Waiting on 3 people' — I know who to ping." But both interviewees flagged shaming risk. Design shows WHO hasn't responded but frames neutrally — "hasn't responded yet" not "is delaying the group."

Core Data Model

Four entities: Trip (name, organizer, status, date ranges, budget, destination, deadline), Member (trip reference, name, status, availability, vote, proxy flag), DateRange (trip reference, start, end, available count), DestinationOption (trip reference, name, note, emoji, vote count). Supabase RLS policies ensure members can only see and modify their own responses within trips they belong to.

Testing Plan

Create a trip with 5 cohort members. Each opens the link on their phone, inputs availability, votes on destination, confirms commitment. Key qualitative measure: does the organizer still need to explain the plan in WhatsApp? If no, the dashboard works. If yes, iterate.

Closing Principle
Every design decision traces back to one belief: the trip should be harder to cancel than to confirm.

If the tool makes deciding effortless and visible, the default shifts from inertia — where trips quietly die — to momentum, where trips happen.
